The Perillo Chair

A pair of posters promoting different aspects of the Perillo Chair



Overview

This project featuring the Perillo Chair explores how posters can contain the same amount of information but emphasize different pieces of information. Both designs contain an image of the chair, its name, the designer's name, and a contextual image. The first poster focuses on the defining features of the futuristic chair - its highly-reflective enamel, odd curvature, and cheeky smile. On the other hand, the second poster emphasizes the typography and the Möbius strip (one of the inspirations for this chair).


Process

Mission

Create two posters for a chair found in the Hunt Library. Both should contain an image of the chair, its name, the designer's name, and a contextual image. One poster should emphasize the defining characteristics of the chair. The other poster should focus on the contextual imagery or typography, but the chair should still be visible.


Playthrough

I began by taking a tour of Hunt Library, looking for interesting chairs to study. Then the Perillo Chair's simplicity and elegant, seamless shape caught my eye. The seat, armrests, and back are all one piece. It smoothly and securely swiveled around. And best of all, it smiled back at you. After doing more research on the lounge chair, I learned that it was designed by Martin Ballendat for the Swiss furniture manufacturer Züco Bürositzmöbel. Furthermore, the form was influenced by that of the Möbius strip , a surface with only one side and one boundary. Futuristic yet stylish, it's intended to give the user a sense of power. I decided that I wanted to convey all these characteristics through my posters.

I spent more time in the library sketching the chair in different positions and compositions. Eventually, I determined the cropping that accentuated its smile, smooth form, shiny surface, and sense of power the most. To emphasize its futuristic nature, I added a Möbius strip, one of its equations, and a (smiling) front-view hologram of the chair floating in the digital void. The gridlines are also meant to make the user think of mathematical graphs. All of the typography is in Courier, a monospaced typeface associated with the electronic world.

Instead of focusing on the chair's form, the second poster shifts the hierarchy to the typography and contextual imagery. The first thing that catches your eye is the Möbius strip - now much brighter - and the typography following the edge of the strip. I stripped the chair of color and gave it a faint green tint, giving it that hologram feel. However, you can still see the basic shape of the chair and the shiny metal on the base.


Results

Both posters have a similar futuristic vibe to them, but the items emphasized in each one differ. In the first one, your eyes are fixated on the high contrast colors of the chair, its gracefulness and sleekness, and its cheeky smile. In the second poster, the Möbius strip is the one screaming for attention, followed by the large type. The chair seems much more timid, partially hidden in the strip.
